Overview

I worked with my group to do the research, and largely led the direction for our general design style and subsequent style guide.

The primary issue stemmed from the disorganized overload of information that users encounter upon visiting the website. It lacked clear readability and appropriate visual cues to seamlessly guide users through the content.

We sought to improved the flow of information on each page, ensuring it is concise and connected. Rather than having separate pages, we have consolidated everything onto fewer pages (volunteer & donate). We have also placed a greater emphasis on presenting their mission, narrative, and specific needs.

Research

We split up interviewing, and doing a competitive analysis, letting us get a good grasp on places to improve for our project, in comparison to similar entities.

We did a heuristic evaluation of the GRIN website to help us know the key points that needed addressed in addition to the general overhaul we planned to implement.

We pulled an affinity diagram from the interview of volunteering individuals, and a stakeholder within GRIN to develop our User Insight Statement.

Definition

Based on our research we defined our Problem Statement:

We are confident that streamlining the page layouts and improving content organization for GRIN will lead to a higher click-through rate and reduced traffic loss. This will ultimately boost volunteer sign-ups and encourage more donor contributions.

In addition we created our Value Proposition:

Our organization, GRIN, is currently developing a user-friendly website navigation and design aimed at making it easier for volunteers and donors to sign up and contribute. We stand out due to our commitment to supporting Gahanna Community residents.

Our credibility stems from our transparent operations and our enduring dedication to providing food to our community, a mission we have been fulfilling for the past 50 years.

Finally using all of our research via interviews and a survey, we collated our Affinity Diagram into our Persona which we then used to create our User Scenarion and move foward with a StoryBoard that mapped out Chris' general experience with our solution.

Ideation

We went through the I Like, I Wish, What If? Ideation activity to come up with ways to address the pain points mentioned in our research, and the overall problem statement we decided on for our project.

We then did a feature prioritization matrix to figure out which of our brainstormed ideas we wanted to run with.

Finally, we began the process of sketching and generally getting our visions down on paper to then combine, starting with designing for Mobile.

Information Architecture

We had to revamp the site layout, which meant reorganizing pages, and event cutting some and pulling their content into others to consolidate page numbers.

Card sorting and a Site Map were key tools in this process, as we reset what pages were going to be within categories, and which categories were even needed in the first place.

Finally, we developed a User Flow that follows Chris through his entire time using the new website, and the many ways he can go about interacting with it.

Testing

Our testing had three phases:

Low-Fidelity User Tests: We found that users were generally able to easily use our prototype, but found that some navigation categories and the footer were odd and could be laid out better.

Mid-Fidelity User Tests: We found that once again users had an easy time using our prototype, found the information organization much more helpful, including the inclusion of footer-like information within the navigation, but they found page length to be long and believe a return to top functionality would be very useful.

High-Fidelity A/B Tests: We tested the different experience users had with the donation flow, accessing it through a button, or through an imbedded form. We found that users noted a more immersive and clean experience when using the button that took them to the exclusive donation flow.
